What is Pymetrics?

Pymetrics is a neuroscience-based assessment platform used by Goldman Sachs, Accenture, Unilever, LinkedIn, and 50+ other firms. Instead of traditional aptitude tests, it uses 12 short games (each 2–5 minutes) to measure 91 cognitive and emotional traits — then matches your profile against top performers at the target company.

💡 Key Insight: Pymetrics doesn't have a single "passing" score. Your profile is compared against a benchmark model built from successful employees at the specific firm you applied to. This means the "ideal" profile differs between Goldman and Unilever.

The 12 Games & What They Measure

  • Balloon Game — Risk tolerance. Pump balloons to earn money, but pop = lose it. Aim for 6–8 pumps consistently.
  • Money Exchange (Ultimatum/Trust/Dictator) — Altruism, trust, fairness. Be genuine — don't try to fake generosity.
  • Tower of London — Planning and executive function. Think 2–3 moves ahead before touching anything.
  • Keypress — Attention and effort. Press the spacebar consistently but not frantically. Rhythm matters.
  • Hard/Easy Task — Need for cognition. Choosing hard tasks signals intellectual curiosity — valuable for consulting roles.
  • Arrows — Response inhibition. Ignore the direction the arrow points — respond to its position. Practice this muscle memory.
  • Lengths — Attention to detail. Compare line lengths carefully. Accuracy over speed.
  • Cards — Learning under uncertainty. Observe which deck gives better returns and switch when patterns change.
  • Faces — Emotional intelligence. Take your time — there's no timer pressure. Don't overthink micro-expressions.
  • Signal/Stop — Impulse control. Stop when you see the stop signal, even mid-keypress.
  • Memory Vault — Working memory. Use chunking — group digits into pairs or triplets.
  • Shape Spinner — Processing speed and visuospatial reasoning. Speed matters here — trust your first instinct.
⚠️ Don't try to game the system. Pymetrics has cross-validation checks across multiple games. If you try to appear unusually generous in Money Exchange but show extreme risk-aversion in Balloon, the algorithm flags the inconsistency.

How Pymetrics Actually Scores You

The algorithm isn't measuring whether you're "good" or "bad" — it's matching your cognitive fingerprint to a model of successful employees. Here's what that means for your strategy.

Pymetrics uses a proprietary AI to build a "success profile" from actual top-performing employees at each company. Your game data is matched against this profile. Arctic Shores: The Science Behind Skyrise Builder

Skyrise Builder is Arctic Shores' flagship game. Understanding what it measures — and why planning depth matters more than speed — can dramatically improve your score.

Skyrise Builder measures your planning horizon — how many steps ahead you can think before committing to an action. The algorithm tracks: number of moves taken, whether you corrected mistakes, time spent before first move (longer = better planning), and deviation from the optimal path. Strategy: spend the first 5–8 seconds studying the full board before touching anything. Mentally simulate 3 moves ahead. The game rewards deliberate players, not fast ones. Correcting a mistake costs you 2x the points of never making it — plan accordingly.

Acing Aon Cut-e Switch Tests

The Switch test is one of the most challenging Aon games. It requires maintaining two competing rules simultaneously — here's the exact mental model that works.

The Aon Switch test presents you with a character and a rule badge. You must sort by one rule when the badge is blue and a different rule when it's orange. The key error most candidates make: reading the character first, then checking the rule. The correct mental model: check the rule badge FIRST, then process the character. Keep your fingers resting on the response keys before the stimulus appears. Maintain a metronome-like rhythm — steady responses score better than bursts of speed followed by errors. Practice alternating between two different classification rules until it becomes automatic.

HireVue Game-Based Assessments: Full Breakdown

Deloitte, BlackRock and Intel all use HireVue games. The 5 games measure spatial reasoning, memory, and numeracy — here's what each one is really testing.

HireVue uses 5 game-based assessments: Numerosity (mental arithmetic speed), Flashback (visual sequence memory), Pathfinder (spatial planning), Shapedance (3D rotation), and Number Dash (divided attention). The most common mistake: treating these like traditional aptitude tests. HireVue games are adaptive — difficulty increases with your score. Don't panic when questions get harder; it means you're performing well. For Shapedance: count blocks along each axis rather than trying to visualise the whole shape rotating. For Pathfinder: work backwards from the exit. For Numerosity: scan the screen for target numbers systematically, starting from top-left.

The Balloon Game: Risk Calibration Strategy

Most candidates either pop everything (too risky) or bank after 1 pump (too cautious). The optimal strategy sits in a precise middle zone — here's exactly what it is.

The Balloon Game has an "optimal" risk zone that most successful candidates naturally hit. The balloon can be pumped between 1 and ~128 times. Pumping 6–10 times per balloon before banking demonstrates calibrated risk tolerance — you're neither reckless nor hyper-conservative. This signals to employers: "This person makes calculated decisions under uncertainty." Practical tips: Start conservatively on the first balloon (3–4 pumps) to gauge the explosion risk. Gradually increase until you find a comfortable rhythm. After a pop, reset to 5–6 pumps on the next balloon — don't let one loss change your strategy dramatically. The algorithm tracks both average pumps AND consistency of decision-making across all balloons.

N-Back Training: How to Improve Working Memory Fast

Working memory is measurable, trainable and used in 60% of recruitment games. This evidence-backed training plan can improve your N-back score in 7 days.

Working memory — your ability to hold and manipulate information in mind — is one of the most commonly measured traits in recruitment games. The N-back task (used by Aon Cut-e and others) requires you to remember what item appeared N steps ago. Research shows that dual N-back training (visual + auditory simultaneously) produces the largest working memory gains. Practical protocol: Practice the N-back game on CognitIQ daily for 15–20 minutes for 7 days. Start at 1-back. Move to 2-back when you hit 85% accuracy. Key mental trick: Don't try to remember the full sequence. Instead, constantly update a "rolling window" — as each new item appears, drop the oldest from your mental buffer and add the new one. Verbalising the sequence quietly (subvocalisation) significantly improves retention.

What Is a Good Score? Understanding Percentile Bands

What does "72% accuracy" actually mean? How do employers interpret your scores? A plain-English guide to percentile bands and what top performers look like.

Raw scores in recruitment games are rarely what employers look at. Instead, your score is benchmarked against all other candidates who took the same assessment — producing a percentile rank. Top 10% (90th percentile): Outstanding — very likely to progress. Top 25% (75th percentile): Strong — competitive. Top 50% (50th percentile): Average — borderline. Below 50%: Likely screened out in high-volume graduate schemes. In our post-game insights, we show you which band your performance falls into. Key insight: accuracy matters more than raw score in most games. A candidate who scores 800 points with 90% accuracy almost always ranks higher than one who scores 1,200 points with 55% accuracy. Speed is secondary to precision in most employer models. Focus on accuracy first, then work on speed.
